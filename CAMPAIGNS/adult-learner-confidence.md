# Adult Learner Confidence Campaign

Last updated: 2026-05-28

## Campaign Goal

Reach adults who want to rebuild reading or math confidence without shame, pressure, or childish language.

## Campaign Name

Start Where You Are

## Simple Promise

It is not too late to practice reading or math.

## Audience

- Adults rebuilding reading confidence
- Adults rebuilding math confidence
- Older learners
- ESL learners
- Job seekers who want stronger daily skills
- Community education programs

## Core Message

Adults do not need babyish practice.

They need clear, respectful, simple lessons that move one step at a time.

ReadEasy30 helps adults practice reading with short passages and calm questions.
MathEasy30 helps adults practice math with simple steps and low pressure.

## Social Post Drafts

### Post 1

It is not too late to practice reading or math.

Start small.
Go slowly.
Repeat tomorrow.

Confidence grows from finished steps.

### Post 2

Adult learners deserve respect.

Simple practice does not mean childish practice.

Clear words. Calm lessons. One step at a time.

### Post 3

If reading feels hard, start with a short passage.

If math feels hard, start with one problem.

Do not start with everything.
Start with one thing.

### Post 4

A good adult practice routine:

Read one short passage.
Answer a few questions.
Practice one math skill.
Review mistakes calmly.

Progress is built by returning.

### Post 5

Shame keeps people stuck.

Practice helps people move.

Start where you are.

## Short Video Scripts

### Script 1: Never too late

Hook: It is not too late to practice reading or math.

Say: Start with one short lesson. Move slowly. Repeat the skill. Do not compare yourself to anyone else.

Close: One calm step still counts.

### Script 2: Simple but respectful

Hook: Adult learners do not need childish lessons.

Say: They need clear steps, useful words, and patient practice. Simple can still be respectful.

Close: Start where you are.

### Script 3: One thing rule

Hook: If learning feels overwhelming, use the one thing rule.

Say: Read one passage or solve one problem. Then stop and review. One finished step is better than ten unfinished starts.

Close: Come back tomorrow.
