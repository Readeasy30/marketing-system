# New Kitten Checklist

Bringing home a kitten is exciting.

It is also a big change for the kitten and the family.

This checklist helps you prepare a safe space, basic supplies, a litter box setup, and a first vet visit plan.

> **Pet safety note:** This page gives general pet-care information. It does not replace a veterinarian. Call your vet if symptoms are serious, sudden, painful, or getting worse.

## Quick Start Checklist

Before your kitten comes home, prepare:

- kitten food
- food and water bowls
- litter box
- kitten-safe litter
- scoop and waste bags
- carrier
- safe sleeping space
- scratching post or pad
- safe toys
- brush or comb
- pet-safe cleaning supplies
- vet appointment
- quiet starter room

## Set Up A Starter Room

A new kitten can feel scared in a full house.

Start with one quiet room if possible.

Include:

- food and water
- litter box away from food
- soft bed or blanket
- safe hiding spot
- toys
- scratching surface

Let the kitten adjust before giving access to the whole home.

## Litter Box Basics

Place the litter box somewhere quiet, easy to reach, and not right next to food and water.

Scoop often.

If the kitten misses the box, do not punish. Check the setup and ask your veterinarian if the problem continues.

## Food And Water

Use food made for kittens unless your veterinarian says otherwise.

Do not change food suddenly. A slow food change can help avoid stomach upset.

Keep clean water available.

## First Vet Visit

Schedule a vet visit soon after bringing your kitten home.

Ask about:

- vaccines
- deworming
- flea prevention
- food amount
- weight and growth
- spay/neuter timing
- microchip options
- litter box issues

Bring shelter, rescue, breeder, or previous-owner records if you have them.

## Kitten-Proof Your Home

Kittens climb, chew, hide, and squeeze into small spaces.

Check for:

- loose cords
- medicine
- cleaning supplies
- small objects
- open windows or loose screens
- reclining chairs
- unsafe plants
- string, ribbon, yarn, or rubber bands
- trash cans

Keep unsafe items out of reach.

## Safe Toys And Scratching

Kittens need play and scratching.

Use toys that are not small enough to swallow.

Avoid leaving string, ribbon, or yarn out when no adult is watching.

Give a scratching post or pad early so the kitten learns where scratching belongs.

## Children And Kittens

Teach children to:

- use gentle hands
- let the kitten hide or rest
- avoid pulling tails, ears, or whiskers
- avoid chasing
- ask an adult before picking up the kitten
- keep doors and windows closed

Adult supervision is important.

## Warning Signs To Watch For

Call your veterinarian if your kitten has:

- repeated vomiting
- repeated diarrhea
- trouble breathing
- refusal to eat
- extreme tiredness
- signs of pain
- swollen belly
- coughing that gets worse
- eye or nose discharge that gets worse
- sudden weakness

## Common Questions

### Where should a kitten sleep the first night?

A quiet starter room with a safe bed, water, litter box, and hiding spot can help the kitten feel secure.

### How soon should a kitten see a vet?

Soon after coming home. A veterinarian can check health, vaccines, parasites, growth, and care needs.

### What if my kitten will not use the litter box?

Check box location, cleanliness, litter type, and stress. If the problem continues, ask your veterinarian.

### Should I let my kitten roam the whole house right away?

Many kittens do better starting in one safe room and earning more space slowly.

## Final Reminder

A kitten needs safety, routine, patience, and vet care.

Start simple. Make the home safe. Ask your veterinarian when you are unsure.
